# Skill: Chapter Craft

A method for writing a strong chapter opening on request. Apply it; output ONLY the prose the task asks for (no preamble, no notes, honor every length cap).

## 1 — Open in the scene, not around it
Start inside a concrete moment — a specific place, an action already under way. No throat-clearing, no backstory dump, no weather-report or "the sun rose" open, no "This is a story about". The first line should create a pull to read on.

## 2 — Earn the entry (freshness)
Avoid the worn openings — a character waking up, "It was a dark and stormy night", "Little did they know". Find a specific, original way in. Specific beats generic; a concrete detail beats an abstract statement.

## 3 — Show, hold POV, keep faith with the facts
- Show state through action, body, and detail rather than naming emotions.
- Hold one POV and one tense unless told otherwise — no head-hopping, no slips.
- Honor any established facts (season, injuries, who's alive) — never contradict them.
- Match the asked-for register (cozy vs hard-boiled vs literary) and write a SCENE, not a plot summary.

## 4 — Tighten ruthlessly (this is where weak drafts lose)
When rewriting or editing, cut hard: kill adjective/adverb pile-ups (keep the one that earns its place), delete redundant phrasing ("small, tiny, little" → one word), and replace weak verb+adverb with one strong verb. PRESERVE the core image and mood — tightening means fewer words doing more work, not stripping the picture. Stay under any word limit.

## Don't over-correct
If a draft already does its job, leave it. Don't add flourish to clean prose or manufacture changes to look busy. Strong, plain writing beats decorated writing.
