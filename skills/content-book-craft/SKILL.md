# Book-craft: drafting prose readers don't put down

A vetted playbook for drafting book chapters and sections — fiction or practical non-fiction.
Apply it whenever you write prose meant to be *read for pleasure or by a paying reader*, not
skimmed. The goal is published-quality drafting on the first pass.

## Open with a hook, never a definition
The first two sentences decide whether the reader continues. Open on something concrete: a
specific moment, a tension, a surprising claim, a question the reader already feels. Never open
with a generic scene-set ("In today's fast-paced world…", "Email has become a part of modern
life…") or a dictionary definition. Drop the reader *into* something.

## One through-line per section; promise then payoff
Each section makes one move. State or imply a promise early ("here's why your inbox betrays
you") and pay it off before the section ends. Every paragraph must advance the through-line — if
a paragraph could be cut without loss, cut it.

## Show with specifics; specificity is credibility
Replace abstract claims with concrete, sensory, situational detail. Not "managing email is
stressful" but "you open your laptop, see 47 unread, and close it again." Use real numbers,
named examples, small physical details. Generic prose reads as filler; concrete prose reads as
true.

## Voice and rhythm
- Active voice, second person for how-to ("you"), consistent POV throughout.
- Vary sentence length deliberately. A short one lands. Then a longer one carries the thought
  forward with momentum and texture. Monotone rhythm is the clearest AI tell.
- Write the way a sharp human talks — contractions, plain words over showy ones.

## End sections with forward pull, not a recap
Close on momentum: a takeaway that points ahead, a question, a turn — not "In summary, we
discussed…". Make the reader want the next paragraph.

## Kill the AI tells
Never use: "In today's fast-paced world", "It's important to note", "Furthermore" / "Moreover"
as crutches, "delve", "tapestry", "testament to", "navigate the landscape", "in the realm of",
"at the end of the day", empty hedges ("arguably", "it could be said"). No throat-clearing
intros ("This chapter will explore…"). No bullet-list filler where prose is wanted. If a
sentence could appear in any book on any topic, rewrite it so it could only appear in *this* one.
